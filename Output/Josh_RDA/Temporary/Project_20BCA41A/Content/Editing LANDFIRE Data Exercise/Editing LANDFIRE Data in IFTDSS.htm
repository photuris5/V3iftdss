Editing LANDFIRE Data in IFTDSS

 

LANDFIRE data are designed to be a course scale satellite-derived national dataset. Due to recent disturbance mechanisms such as fire, windthrow, beetle, drought and disease outbreak, as well as management activities, LANDFIRE data may not reflect well the actual fuel conditions in your project area. Consequently, LANDFIRE data may need to be edited to better reflect the local conditions within your project area, principally to reflect anticipated fire behavior. Additionally, as fuel treatments are simulated LANDFIRE data will need to be edited to reflect post-treatment conditions. Editing a LANDFIRE data set is much easier and faster than developing a landscape data file from scratch.

Editing the Landscape Exercise 1

 

In this first exercise, you're going to make a simple edit to this landscape you just created to simulate a prolonged drought. You’re going to change the GR2 to a GR1, which is a short, sparse, dry climate grass.

 

  1. Return to your browser tab that has My Workspace open.
  2. Select your landscape and choose Edit. The Edit Landscape page will appear. The starting landscape has defaulted to your new landscape, however you could choose others from the drop-down box. You may choose to click on the map/arrow symbol at the top of the page and split your view so you can see both the editing page and the map with your landscape displayed in one view.
  3. Select Add User Created Edit Rule.
  4. Where these conditions exist click, add a row.
  5. From the Attribute spinner box choose Fuel Model.
  6. From the Operator box choose is equal to.
  7. From the Value box choose 102(GR2).

               The editor will now find all cells on the landscape that have a fuel model equal to 102(GR2). You may remember from using the identify tool that fuel model 102 comprises the majority of the landscape.

  1. Move down to modify the following values and click add row.
  2. Choose Fuel Model for Attribute.
  3. Choose set to from the Modifier and choose 101 (GR1).

              You are not going to be applying a landscape mask so we are done creating the rule.

  1. Select Add to Rules.

               This will add the rule to Landscape Edit Rule list above. Your rule should read, “Where (Fuel Model is equal to 102) change (Fuel Model set to 101)”.

  1. Enter a Name for the new edited landscape you are about to create.

               You may name it anything you choose; I named mine “Grassy Valley Drought." 

  1. Choose My Workspace tab from the top of the page to return to your workspace.
  2. Choose your project folder.

               You should see the newly created landscape.

  1. Open your project folder and click on the name of the new landscape and choose Request a Report.
  2. When the report is complete choose View Summary.
  3. Scroll to Fuel Model

               You should see your new landscape still has only two fuel models, but it is mostly GR1 and a little SH1. If you remember your original landscape was mostly GR2 so this tells you that your edit was effective changing GR2 to GR1.

Editing the Landscape

For this edit exercise, we are going to treat this like it is a project that we are going to develop treatment alternatives for. 

  1. Select the Cycle tab from the top of the page.
  2. Choose the Strategic Planning pedal from the right side of the cycle wheel.
  3. Select Develop Treatment Alternatives box from the right of the wheel.
  4. Select your newly created landscape from the Select Landscape Box.
  5. Choose Cruches Basin Wilderness for the Area of Interest You may generate or open a landscape report or view your landscape in the “Map Studio” by choosing the buttons to the right.
  6. Select the Edit Landscape tab.

               You will notice this is the same interface you used to edit the other landscape.

  1. Choose Cruches Basin as your starting landscape and choose Add User Created Edit Rule.

               Assume fuels represented by TU1 161 are aspen stands that recently experienced high mortality particularly on the dry south-facing slopes. The aspen stands on the southerly aspects now have many jackstrawed dead down logs that are dry enough to burn.

  1. Select add a row and choose Fuel Model for the Attribute select is equal to for the Operator and 161 (TU1) for the Value.
  2. Add another row and select Aspect for Attribute and is greater than or equal to for the Operator and 113 degrees for the value.
  3. Add one more row and choose Aspect again, choose is less than or equal to for the Operator and 292 for the value.

               This will cause the editor to find all cells that are fuel model 161 (TU1) and are on an aspect between 113 and 292 degrees.

  1. Go down to Modify the following values and add a row and choose Canopy Cover choose multiply by for Modifier and add a value of 0.65.

               This will reduce canopy cover for cells that meet the above criterion by 35% to simulate the loss of standing live trees. Canopy cover will affect midflame wind speeds.

  1. Add another row and choose Fuel Model choose set to for the Modifier and 184 (TL4) for the Value.

               This will change the cells that meet the above criterion to fuel model 184 (TL4), which are small downed logs. You may consider modifying canopy bulk density as well, but this is at low value already, and we are not concerned that live aspen crowns will burn. If crown fire were a concern, then this value should be edited as well.

  1. Choose Cruches Basin for Landscape Mask.

               Because we are only concerned with Cruches Basin Wilderness, we are going to choose Cruches Basin Wilderness as a Landscape Mask. This will limit the edits to the wilderness area only.

  1. Select Add to Rules.

               You will see your new rule added to Landscape Edit Rule list above. You may notice the editor has changed the order of your rows, but the important thing is, does the rule language make sense?
