Getting Started and Comparing Treatment Alternatives

In this hypothetical scenario, you have been tasked with altering LANDFIRE data in to reflect your landscape, and compare the potential effect of two fuel treatment alternatives on fire behavior under 97th percentile weather conditions for your site.

This tutorial will walk through the steps of creating, evaluating, and comparing fuels treatments on an example landscape. We will assume you have just logged into IFTDSS for the first time, and will proceed through these operations from start to finish. In this tutorial you will:

  • Begin a project
  • Create a landscape
  • Evaluate your landscape and edit it to reflects the currents conditions on the ground using the Landscape Evaluation stage of the planning cycle
  • Identify a priority treatment area using the Auto97th fire behavior summary and examining wildland urban interface areas in Map Studio
  • Create treatment polygon in
  • Simulate a fuels treatments and compare their predicted effect on fire behavior using the Develop Treatment Alternatives task in the Strategic Planning stage of the planning cycle .

We provide all the information needed to complete this tutorial; However, you may choose to use your own area or files as well.

If you are unfamiliar with IFTDSS, we recommend watching the 4 minute tour to familiarize yourself with how IFTDSS is laid out.

Create a landscape

You can create a landscape in IFTDSS in multiple ways. For example, you could go right to and create a landscape using the Landscape Tools. But for this tutorial we’ll create a new landscape by going into the Planning Cycle, Landscape Evaluation, and Landscape Summary.

On created we can view the landscape, but hold off on looking at the landscape summary, because we know we’ll want to make some edits to the landscape for this tutorial.

We will look at a portion of the Tahoe National Forest located at:

  • West: -120.8856 degrees
  • East: -120.6044 degrees
  • South: 39.4463 degrees
  • North: 39.6149 degrees

To help keep track of your data, we recommend including the landfire version in the name. For the example landscape we are about to create, we will name it: 'HaypressCrk LF2012 Unedited'

Identify a priority treatment area using the Auto97th fire behavior summary and examining Wildland Urban Interface Areas in Map Studio

To identify the areas that would be good candidates for treatment, we’ll create a summary report using the Auto 97th Summary, this will automatically populate a Landscape Fire Behavior model with 97th percentile moisture conditions based on the Remote Automatic Weather Station (RAWS) nearest our area. This will generate both a report, and a mapped output. We'll look at the mapped output.

To begin, go back to the Landscape Evaluation stage of the planning cycle, select the Landscape Summary task: 

For your Landscape, specify a starting landscape to represent fie bahavior on an untreated landscape as it currently exists. For this tutorial, we'll select the Existing Condition landscape we just created: 'HaypressCrkLF2012EC'

Once your summary is created, navigate to My Workspace, and view the fire behavior outputs in by selecting the 'Haypress LF2012 EC - Auto97th model output file. Use the Layer List and Zoom to evaluate fire behavior across your landscape. For this example we'll focus on the Sierra City wildand urban interface in the northeast area of the map.

As you evaluate your landscape you may find it helpful to adjust the layer transparency using the more options button to the right of your fire behavior layer, and the different basemap gallery options.

Toggling through the fire behavior attributes of flame length, and canopy fire, we can see some areas north of town, and east between the North Yuba River and some scattered structures that could make good candidates for treatment. In this example you'll notice the circled areas indicate potentially high flame lengths, rates of spread, active crown fire, and a max spread direction moving north to northeast.

You may also use the Landscape Tools button to add the landscape file to , and the Swipe tool to view the different layers.

In the next steps, we'll create shapefiles for these areas, then use those shapefiles to specify which part of the landscape to simulate our treatment alternatives for comparison.

Creating Treatment Polygons

To simulate and evaluate the impact of treatments, we'll delineate our planned treatment areas with shapefiles in . First we will create a polygon for each treatment area, next we will combine those into one shapefile so we can use the combined file in our comparison.

First, we'll create three individual shape files:

Open Create/Edit Shapes , select your method, in this case the freehand draw method, and draw your polygon.

You can use the delete button to get rid of the polygon before saving, or edit it by single left clicking and moving the vertices. When you are satisfied with your first polygon, give it a name, select a project folder and click Create. Once created, polygons will appear on the map in purple. Continue this for the three treatment areas as shown below.

Repeat this step for two additional treatment areas, similar to those in the image above.

To combine these into one file, select the Copy from Existing Features button , make sure the Select multipart option under the button is selected, and left mouse click your first polygon. It will appear highlighted once selected. Continue this process, each time selecting the Copy from Existing Features button , and highlighting the next polygon, until all are selected. When finished, give the set a name, specify a folder to save it in (in this case our Haypress Crk Project folder), and select Create.

Once the new shapefile has been successfully created, you will see 'Shape Created' Appear in Create/Edit Shapes.

Shapes are available in My Workspace in the folder you selected (in this case the Haypress Creek Project folder).
